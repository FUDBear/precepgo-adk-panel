## 1. AIRWAY MANAGEMENT (Chapter 28)

### Management Topic: Airway Assessment and Planning
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Thorough airway-relevant history and physical examination
- Mallampati scoring, thyromental distance, mouth opening assessment
- Ultrasound for cricothyroid membrane identification
- Risk stratification for difficult laryngoscopy, difficult mask ventilation
- Multiple predictive indices (Mallampati, sternomental distance, upper lip bite test)

**Clinical Scenarios Requiring Specific Management:**
1. **Anticipated difficult airway** - Requires awake intubation, fiberoptic techniques
2. **Unanticipated difficult airway** - Follow ASA difficult airway algorithm
3. **Full stomach/aspiration risk** - Rapid sequence induction considerations
4. **Cannot intubate, cannot ventilate (CICV)** - Emergency cricothyroidotomy
5. **Failed supraglottic airway** - Transition to emergency airway access

**Patient Characteristics Creating Management Challenges:**
- Obesity (BMI >35 kg/m2) - Reduced apnea tolerance, difficult mask ventilation
- Limited mouth opening (<4 cm) - Difficult blade insertion
- Presence of beard - 3.18 odds ratio for difficult mask ventilation
- History of head/neck radiation - Friable tissue, limited mobility
- Congenital syndromes (Pierre Robin, Treacher Collins, Down syndrome)
- Limited cervical spine mobility - Restricted positioning for laryngoscopy
- Morbid obesity with OSA - Rapid desaturation, difficult ventilation
- Pregnancy - Aspiration risk, anatomic airway changes
- Advanced age (>55 years) - 2.26 odds ratio for difficult mask ventilation

---

### Management Topic: Preoxygenation and Apneic Oxygenation
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Denitrogenation with 100% O2 for 3-5 minutes
- Vital capacity technique (4 breaths over 30 seconds)
- Modified vital capacity (8 deep breaths in 60 seconds)
- Apneic oxygenation via nasal cannula (3-60 L/min)
- High-flow nasal oxygen (THRIVE technique for prolonged apnea)
- Bilevel positive airway pressure in obese patients
- Reverse Trendelenburg positioning

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Obese patient** - May desaturate in 2.7 minutes vs 6 minutes for non-obese
2. **Rapid sequence induction** - Critical for maximizing apnea time
3. **Anticipated difficult intubation** - Prolonged attempts require oxygen reserve
4. **Pediatric patients** - Reduced functional residual capacity
5. **Pregnant patients** - Increased oxygen consumption, reduced FRC

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Reduced functional residual capacity
- Pulmonary disease - Reduced oxygen stores, increased shunting
- Claustrophobia - Poor mask tolerance
- Facial hair/anatomy - Mask seal difficulties, air entrainment
- Pregnancy - Increased metabolic demand

---

### Management Topic: Facemask Ventilation
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Proper mask seal technique (thumb-index finger grip)
- Jaw thrust maneuver
- Two-handed mask ventilation
- Oral and nasal airway adjuncts
- Sniffing position for optimal alignment
- Appropriate ventilation pressure (20-25 cm H2O)
- Expiratory chin drop maneuver

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Cannot ventilate after induction** - Requires immediate troubleshooting
2. **Laryngospasm** - CPAP, deepening anesthesia, paralysis
3. **Obese/edentulous patient** - Two-person technique needed
4. **Bearded patient** - Mask seal challenges
5. **Pediatric airway obstruction** - Age-appropriate sizing

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Presence of beard (3.18 OR for difficulty)
- BMI >26 kg/m2 (2.75 OR)
- Lack of teeth (2.28 OR)
- Age >55 years (2.26 OR)
- History of snoring (1.84 OR)
- Limited mandibular protrusion
- Mallampati III/IV

---

### Management Topic: Supraglottic Airways (SGAs)
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- LMA insertion technique (Brain technique)
- Proper sizing and cuff pressure management (<60 cm H2O)
- First-generation vs second-generation devices
- Flexible LMA for shared airway cases
- LMA removal timing (deep vs awake)
- Positive pressure ventilation limits (typically <20 cm H2O)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Failed intubation, successful ventilation** - SGA as rescue device
2. **Shared airway surgery** (ENT, ophthalmology) - Flexible LMA
3. **Bronchospasm risk** - SGA preferred over ETT
4. **Bailey maneuver** - SGA for emergence in bronchospasm patients
5. **Cardiopulmonary resuscitation** - Reduced regurgitation vs bag-mask

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Full stomach - Aspiration risk (contraindication for elective use)
- Limited mouth opening (<1.5 cm) - Insertion difficulty
- High airway resistance - Inadequate seal
- Glottic/subglottic obstruction - Device ineffective
- Hiatal hernia with significant GERD - Increased aspiration risk

---

### Management Topic: Tracheal Intubation
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Direct laryngoscopy with Macintosh/Miller blades
- Videolaryngoscopy (VL) techniques
- Rapid sequence induction protocol
- Sniffing position vs neutral position
- BURP maneuver (backward, upward, rightward pressure)
- Bougie/introducer use
- Confirmation: End-tidal CO2 (gold standard)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Rapid sequence induction** - Full stomach, minimize aspiration risk
2. **Failed direct laryngoscopy** - Transition to VL or alternate technique
3. **Cervical spine precautions** - Manual in-line stabilization
4. **Obesity/OSA** - Ramped positioning
5. **Anticipated difficult airway** - Awake intubation techniques

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Limited neck extension - Poor glottic visualization
- Mallampati >1 with risk factors - 2-17% difficult intubation rate
- Scarring/radiation/masses in neck - Failed VL predictors
- Small mouth opening - Blade insertion difficulty
- Otolaryngology/cardiac surgery patients - Higher VL difficulty

---

### Management Topic: Awake Intubation
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Topical anesthesia (lidocaine spray, atomized, nebulized)
- Superior laryngeal nerve block
- Glossopharyngeal nerve block
- Transtracheal injection
- Flexible fiberoptic intubation
- Sedation with dexmedetomidine, remifentanil
- Maintaining patient cooperation and oxygenation

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Known difficult airway** - Safest approach
2. **Cannot intubate, cannot ventilate risk** - Preserve spontaneous ventilation
3. **Cervical spine instability** - Maintain neurologic monitoring
4. **Airway masses/tumors** - Avoid airway collapse
5. **Morbid obesity with difficult airway** - Awake technique preferred

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Uncooperative patients - Requires excellent preparation and communication
- Bleeding in airway - Obscures view
- Limited mouth opening - Fiber optic challenges
- Severe anxiety - May require increased sedation
- Active airway reflexes - Inadequate topicalization

---

### Management Topic: Emergency Airway Access
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Cricothyroidotomy (surgical vs needle)
- Identification of cricothyroid membrane
- Ultrasound-guided landmark identification
- Emergency front-of-neck access protocols
- Scalpel-bougie-tube technique

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Cannot intubate, cannot oxygenate (CICO)** - Life-threatening emergency
2. **Massive airway bleeding** - Upper airway inaccessible
3. **Laryngeal trauma** - Distorted anatomy
4. **Angioedema** - Complete upper airway obstruction
5. **Foreign body obstruction** - Unable to remove/bypass

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Difficult landmark identification (only 33% correctly identify CTM)
- Short neck - Reduced workspace
- Previous neck surgery/radiation - Distorted anatomy
- Pediatric patients - Small anatomic structures
- Emergency/trauma - Limited preparation time

## 2. PATIENT POSITIONING (Chapter 29)

### Management Topic: Supine Positioning
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Horizontal vs contoured (lawn chair) position
- Padding of pressure points (occiput, elbows, heels)
- Arm positioning (tucked vs abducted <90 degrees)
- Prevention of brachial plexus stretch injury
- Neutral head position to avoid lateral neck flexion

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Standard surgical procedures** - Most common position
2. **Lithotomy position** - Perineal/urologic/gynecologic access
3. **Aortocaval compression** - Pregnant patients, large abdominal masses
4. **Long procedures** - Pressure point monitoring
5. **Steep Trendelenburg** - Shoulder brace placement considerations

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Increased pressure points, positioning difficulty
- Pregnancy - Aortocaval compression syndrome
- Limited joint mobility - Cannot tolerate position
- Pre-existing neuropathy - Increased risk of injury
- Cachexia - Minimal soft tissue padding

---

### Management Topic: Prone Positioning
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Proper head positioning to avoid eye pressure
- Chest rolls to allow ventilation
- Genitalia and breast positioning
- Arm positioning to prevent brachial plexus injury
- Prevention of abdominal compression
- Vision loss prevention protocols

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Spine surgery** - High risk for vision loss
2. **Long procedures (>6 hours)** - Pressure injury risk
3. **Posterior fossa craniotomy** - Head positioning critical
4. **Percutaneous procedures** - Shorter duration
5. **Emergency conversion to prone** - Airway secured first

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Difficult positioning, increased abdominal pressure
- Cardiovascular disease - Hemodynamic changes
- Respiratory disease - Ventilation challenges
- Pre-existing eye disease - Vision loss risk
- Large breasts/genitalia - Positioning challenges

---

### Management Topic: Lithotomy Positioning
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Standard, low, high, and exaggerated lithotomy
- Simultaneous leg raising/lowering
- Hip flexion angle monitoring (<90 degrees preferred)
- Popliteal space padding
- Compartment syndrome prevention

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Gynecologic surgery** - Standard lithotomy
2. **Urologic procedures** - Low lithotomy
3. **Perineal surgery** - Variable positioning
4. **Combined abdominal-perineal** - Low lithotomy for dual access
5. **Prolonged lithotomy** - Compartment syndrome risk

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Difficult positioning, increased compartment syndrome risk
- Limited hip flexion - Cannot achieve position
- Vascular disease - Perfusion concerns in high lithotomy
- Previous lower extremity surgery - Position intolerance
- Supermorbid obesity (BMI >50) - Extreme positioning challenges

---

### Management Topic: Lateral Positioning
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Axillary roll placement
- Dependent arm positioning
- Head and neck alignment
- Padding of dependent pressure points
- Bean bag or positioning devices
- Kidney position for renal surgery

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Thoracic surgery** - Lung isolation required
2. **Hip surgery** - Requires stable positioning
3. **Kidney surgery** - Table flexion
4. **Lateral approach spine surgery** - Minimize rotation
5. **Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery** - Precise positioning

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Instability, pressure points
- Kyphoscoliosis - Positioning challenges
- Limited shoulder mobility - Arm positioning difficulty
- Large breasts - Dependent breast positioning
- Pre-existing neuropathy - Increased injury risk

## 3. MONITORED ANESTHESIA CARE (MAC) (Chapter 30)

### Management Topic: Sedation Levels and Conversion
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Continuum of sedation (minimal, moderate, deep, general anesthesia)
- Titration of sedatives (propofol, midazolam, dexmedetomidine)
- Airway management readiness
- Conversion to general anesthesia protocols
- Patient-controlled sedation techniques
- Target-controlled infusion systems

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Local anesthesia with sedation** - Common ambulatory procedures
2. **Regional anesthesia supplementation** - Improved patient comfort
3. **Painful procedural sedation** - Endoscopy, cardioversion
4. **Uncooperative patient** - Conversion to general anesthesia
5. **Airway obstruction during sedation** - Immediate intervention required

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- ASA III-V patients - Higher complication rates
- Elderly (>70 years) - Increased sensitivity to sedatives
- OSA - Airway obstruction, hypoxemia risk
- Obesity - Difficult airway, oxygen desaturation
- Chronic pain/opioid use - Higher sedation requirements
- Anxiety disorders - Difficult to achieve adequate sedation
- Pulmonary hypertension - Cannot tolerate respiratory depression

---

### Management Topic: MAC Candidacy Assessment
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Patient factors (NPO status, aspiration risk, airway assessment)
- Procedure factors (duration, pain level, airway access)
- Surgeon factors (tolerance for movement, conversion willingness)
- Fire risk assessment with supplemental oxygen
- Positioning tolerance evaluation

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Cataract surgery in ASA IV patient** - Topical anesthesia, minimal sedation
2. **Shoulder surgery with interscalene block** - Supplemental MAC
3. **Colonoscopy in high-risk patient** - Careful sedation titration
4. **Breast biopsy** - Local with light sedation
5. **Failed regional anesthesia** - Conversion required

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Morbid obesity with GERD - High aspiration risk
- Severe anxiety - May not cooperate
- Cognitive dysfunction/dementia - Cannot follow commands
- Persistent cough - Procedure interference
- Inability to lie flat - Positioning limitations
- Language barriers - Communication difficulties
- Hearing impairment - Cannot respond to commands

---

### Management Topic: Respiratory Management During MAC
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Supplemental oxygen delivery (nasal cannula, face tent)
- Capnography monitoring during sedation
- Recognition of respiratory depression
- Airway rescue techniques (jaw thrust, oral airway)
- Reversal agents (naloxone, flumazenil)
- Ventilation support readiness

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Respiratory depression from opioids** - Naloxone administration
2. **Upper airway obstruction** - Immediate airway maneuvers
3. **Apnea during deep sedation** - Assisted ventilation
4. **Hypercapnia accumulation** - Reduce sedation, support ventilation
5. **Oxygen desaturation** - Multiple intervention levels

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- OSA - Prone to obstruction and desaturation
- Obesity - Rapid desaturation
- COPD - Baseline hypercapnia, oxygen-induced hypoventilation
- Pulmonary hypertension - Cannot tolerate hypoxia
- Chronic opioid use - Tolerance with respiratory sensitivity paradox

---

### Management Topic: Fire Safety During MAC
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Oxygen-enriched environment recognition
- Fire triangle: oxygen, ignition source, fuel
- Alcohol prep drying time
- Electrocautery safety protocols
- Oxygen delivery modifications (intermittent vs continuous)
- Fire emergency response protocols

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Facial surgery with cautery** - High fire risk zone
2. **Oropharyngeal procedures** - Oxygen accumulation under drapes
3. **Alcohol prep with immediate cautery** - Ignition source
4. **Laser surgery** - Special protocols required
5. **Nasal/airway surgery** - Direct oxygen interface with surgical site

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Oxygen-dependent patients - Cannot reduce FiO2
- Facial hair - Additional fuel source
- Heavy draping required - Oxygen accumulation
- Prolonged surgery - Extended risk exposure

## 4. AMBULATORY ANESTHESIA (Chapter 31)

### Management Topic: Patient Selection for Ambulatory Surgery
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- ASA physical status considerations (ASA I-IV appropriate if stable)
- Age considerations (PCA <46 weeks exclude, >60 weeks require monitoring)
- Procedure complexity assessment
- Enhanced recovery protocols
- Discharge criteria (PADSS scoring)
- Social support requirements

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **ASA III with optimized comorbidities** - Appropriate for low-risk procedures
2. **Ex-premature infant** - Apnea monitoring requirements
3. **OSA patient for low-risk surgery** - With regional anesthesia
4. **Super morbidly obese (BMI >50)** - Requires careful screening
5. **Elderly patient (>80)** - Increased 30-day admission risk

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Post-conceptual age <60 weeks (preterm) or <46 weeks (term) - Apnea risk
- ASA IV with unstable disease - Not appropriate
- Super morbid obesity - Requires excellent pre-screening
- OSA with high opioid requirements - Extended monitoring needed
- Chronic renal failure - Increased 30-day admission risk
- COPD - Higher complications without regional anesthesia
- Active cancer treatment - Increased admission risk
- Uncontrolled diabetes - Perioperative management challenges
- No responsible adult at home - Cannot discharge safely

---

### Management Topic: Anesthetic Technique Selection
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Regional vs general anesthesia (equally safe)
- Peripheral nerve blocks for postoperative analgesia
- Short-acting agents (desflurane, sevoflurane, propofol)
- Multimodal analgesia
- PONV prophylaxis protocols
- Neuromuscular blockade reversal (sugammadex considerations)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Orthopedic surgery** - Regional block primary or supplemental
2. **Laparoscopic surgery** - General anesthesia with PONV prophylaxis
3. **Upper extremity surgery** - Brachial plexus block
4. **Lower extremity surgery** - Sciatic/femoral blocks
5. **High PONV risk patient** - Regional technique preferred

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- PONV risk factors - Major cause of delayed discharge
- High pain anticipated - Nerve blocks improve satisfaction
- Obesity - Regional techniques reduce complications
- OSA - Minimize opioids, use regional
- Pediatric patients - Regional under general anesthesia

---

### Management Topic: Postoperative Nausea and Vomiting
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Risk stratification (Apfel score)
- Multimodal prophylaxis (2-3 antiemetics)
- Avoiding emetogenic agents (etomidate, nitrous oxide)
- Adequate hydration
- Regional anesthesia to reduce opioids
- Rescue antiemetic protocols

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **High-risk patient (4 risk factors)** - 80% PONV risk without prophylaxis
2. **Pediatric strabismus surgery** - Very high risk
3. **Laparoscopic gynecologic surgery** - Multiple risk factors
4. **Breast surgery** - High baseline risk
5. **Ambulatory surgery delay** - PONV most common cause

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Female gender - Doubled risk
- Non-smoker - Increased risk
- History of PONV/motion sickness - Strong predictor
- Postoperative opioid requirement - Dose-dependent risk
- Young age - Pediatric risk factors differ
- Prolonged surgery - Increased risk

---

### Management Topic: Pain Management and Discharge
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Multimodal analgesia strategy
- Peripheral nerve blocks (single injection or continuous)
- NSAIDs and acetaminophen
- Opioid-sparing techniques
- Local infiltration analgesia
- Discharge criteria assessment (PADSS)
- Patient education for home management

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Continuous peripheral nerve catheter** - Home-going protocols
2. **Failed pain control** - Most common cause of admission
3. **Liposomal bupivacaine infiltration** - Extended analgesia
4. **Multimodal regimen** - Preemptive analgesia
5. **Opioid-naive patient** - Careful prescribing

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Chronic opioid use - Higher requirements, tolerance
- Opioid-naive - Risk of oversedation at home
- OSA - Limit opioids
- Renal insufficiency - NSAID contraindications
- Elderly - Increased sensitivity to opioids
- History of substance abuse - Complex pain management

## 7. NEURAXIAL ANESTHESIA (Chapter 35)

### Management Topic: Spinal Anesthesia
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Lumbar puncture technique (midline vs paramedian)
- Local anesthetic selection and dosing
- Baricity considerations (hyper-, hypo-, isobaric)
- Level assessment and dermatomal testing
- Hemodynamic management (fluid preload, vasopressors)
- Complication prevention (PDPH, TNS, hypotension)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Cesarean section** - T4 level required, hemodynamic management critical
2. **Lower extremity surgery** - L2-L4 level adequate
3. **Transurethral procedures** - Low spinal (S2-S4)
4. **Hip fracture in elderly** - Neuraxial may improve outcomes
5. **Failed spinal** - Repeat vs alternative technique

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Difficult landmarks, increased dose requirements
- Scoliosis - Technical difficulty, paramedian approach
- Previous spine surgery - Distorted anatomy
- Coagulopathy - Contraindication
- Fixed cardiac output states - Cannot tolerate afterload reduction
- Hypovolemia - Severe hypotension risk
- Severe aortic stenosis - Traditional contraindication
- Increased intracranial pressure - Relative contraindication

---

### Management Topic: Epidural Anesthesia
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Loss of resistance technique (air vs saline)
- Catheter threading and securing
- Test dose administration (lidocaine with epinephrine)
- Incremental dosing protocols
- Level assessment and top-up techniques
- Continuous infusion management
- Removal timing (consider coagulation status)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Labor epidural analgesia** - Dilute LA/opioid mixtures
2. **Thoracic epidural for major surgery** - ERAS protocol component
3. **Postoperative pain management** - Continuous infusion
4. **Failed epidural block** - Unilateral, patchy, or inadequate
5. **Epidural hematoma prevention** - Coagulation management

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Obesity - Increased depth, difficult landmarks
- Anticoagulation - Timing of placement and removal critical
- Thrombocytopenia - Risk assessment required
- Spinal stenosis - May worsen neurologic symptoms
- Previous back surgery - Technical difficulty
- Epidural space abnormalities - Unpredictable spread
- Infection at insertion site - Contraindication

---

### Management Topic: Combined Spinal-Epidural (CSE)
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Needle-through-needle technique
- Separate space technique
- Advantages of rapid onset with extended duration
- Intrathecal dosing with epidural catheter backup
- Testing epidural catheter functionality

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Labor analgesia with unpredictable duration** - Ideal application
2. **Long orthopedic procedures** - Guaranteed block with extension capability
3. **Cesarean section** - Options for inadequate block
4. **Bilateral lower extremity surgery** - Extended duration needs

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**

- In-plane vs out-of-plane needle visualization

- Local anesthetic spread visualization

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**

- Previous surgery - Scar tissue, distorted anatomy
- Vascular anomalies - Difficult identification

---

### Management Topic: Upper Extremity Blocks
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Interscalene block (shoulder, proximal humerus)
- Supraclavicular block (entire arm, fastest onset)
- Infraclavicular block (elbow, forearm, hand)
- Axillary block (forearm and hand)
- Selective nerve blocks (radial, median, ulnar)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Shoulder arthroscopy** - Interscalene block
2. **Forearm fracture fixation** - Supraclavicular block
3. **Carpal tunnel release** - Axillary or selective blocks
4. **Hand surgery** - Wrist blocks possible
5. **Total shoulder arthroplasty** - Interscalene with catheter

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Contralateral phrenic nerve dysfunction - Interscalene contraindicated
- Severe COPD - Avoid interscalene (hemi-diaphragm paralysis)
- Neurologic deficits pre-existing - Documentation critical
- Obesity - Landmark difficulty
- Previous clavicle fracture - Anatomy distorted

---

### Management Topic: Lower Extremity Blocks
**Key Techniques/Concepts:**
- Femoral nerve block (anterior thigh, knee)
- Sciatic nerve block (posterior thigh, entire leg below knee)
- Popliteal approach to sciatic
- Adductor canal block (saphenous nerve)
- Ankle blocks (tibial, deep peroneal, superficial peroneal, sural, saphenous)
- Lumbar plexus block (paravertebral approach)

**Clinical Scenarios:**
1. **Total knee arthroplasty** - Adductor canal block (preserves quadriceps)
2. **Ankle/foot surgery** - Popliteal sciatic block
3. **Femur fracture** - Femoral nerve block for analgesia
4. **Hip fracture** - Fascia iliaca or femoral block
5. **Forefoot surgery** - Ankle blocks

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**
- Anticoagulation - Lumbar plexus block contraindicated (deep, non-compressible)
- Vascular disease - Poor perfusion with nerve block-induced vasodilation
- Diabetes with neuropathy - Pre-existing deficit documentation
- Obesity - Deep nerve location, difficult landmarks
- Femur fracture - Distorted anatomy, patient positioning difficulty

- Erector spinae plane (ESP) block
- Paravertebral block

**Clinical Scenarios:**

**Patient Characteristics Creating Challenges:**

- Ascites - Fluid distorts planes
- Severe kyphoscoliosis - Paravertebral space abnormal
- Anticoagulation - Paravertebral block has epidural space communication risk
